A Dharma Talk

The Four Notions

Thich Nhat Hanh
🎧 Listen to the full talk
~27 minutes
This teaching draws on the Diamond Sutra's four notions (self, person, living being, lifespan), the Kaccānagotta Sutta on right view beyond being and non-being, and core Buddhist concepts including the skandhas, āyatanas, dhātus, nirvāṇa, eternalism, and nihilism. So when René Descartes said, "I think, therefore I am," he was trying to establish something. He was trying to convince us that the self exists: I think, therefore I am. This is an argument that since thinking is there, there must be a thinker. So he wants to prove that the self exists, because if the action is there, there must be an actor.

This is the opposite of the teaching of the Buddha. In the Buddha's teaching, there is thinking, but there is no thinker. Thinking without a thinker. Thought without a thinker. You do not need a thinker in order for thought to be possible. You do not need a rainer in order for rain to be possible. You do not need a blower in order for the wind to blow. But we have a deep belief that there is a self that continues, always unchanged, while everything else is changing.

The five skandhas, the twelve āyatanas, the eighteen dhātus—they are always changing, every moment. But we imagine that there is something hidden behind them all that continues to be the same, and that is the self. In the teaching of the Buddha, such a thing does not exist.

So in the conventional realm of truth, we think that if there is thought, there must be a thinker; if there is rain, there must be a rainer. But when we observe deeply, we see that we do not need an owner, an actor, or a receiver—the receiver of the contribution, the receiver of the fruit of action.

◆ ◆ ◆

There is a sutra about this. The Buddha was going on an alms round with the monks, and he was stopped by a practitioner of another tradition, an ascetic practitioner. They did not wear any clothes; they wanted to live as naturally as possible and felt they did not need clothing. He stopped the Buddha because he wanted to ask something about the Buddha's teaching. The Buddha at first said that it was not a convenient moment, but the ascetic insisted three times, saying, "My question is very short." So the Buddha agreed to answer.

The man asked, "What do you say, Gautama? Is the one who acts the same as the one who receives the fruit of action? Suppose you do something today, and tomorrow or the day after, who is it that receives the result? Is the person who reaps the fruit of action the same person who acted?"

And the Buddha said, "I do not answer this question." He did not answer because it was not a good question.

Then the ascetic asked in another way: "Do you think that the one who acts and the one who receives the fruit of action are two different persons?" And the Buddha said, "No, I do not answer that question either."

So the first question was whether the one who acts and the one who receives the fruit of action are the same. The Buddha said, "I do not answer." Then he asked the second question: does that mean that the one who acts and the one who receives the retribution are two different persons? The Buddha again said no.

The ascetic then asked, "What does this mean? When I ask whether the one who acts and the one who experiences the result are the same, you say that question should not be answered. And when I ask whether the one who acts and another one experiences the result, you also say that you cannot answer. What does that mean?"

And this is the Buddha's answer: to declare that the one who acts and the one who receives are exactly the same is to fall into the eternalist view. And to declare that the one who acts and the one who experiences the result are two entirely different persons is to fall into the nihilistic view. So, avoiding these two extremes, the Tathāgata teaches the Dharma by the Middle Way.

This sutra shows us that there is a pair of views that must be transcended: the same and the different. The one who acts and the one who receives the fruit of action are neither the same nor different. So sameness and otherness are two notions that we have to remove.

◆ ◆ ◆

This is another aspect of the teaching. When we observe reality as it is, we see that everything is changing all the time, and there is nothing that can remain exactly the same. So there is no self at all. A self would have to be something that remains the same all the time. If you believe that the self is something that remains the same all the time, you fall into a view called eternalism. And if you say the opposite in an absolute way, you fall into the trap of nihilism.

In one of the Paramārtha Gāthās, we read: "Living beings" is the name of a continuous stream. Looking deeply, you do not see any permanent self inside living beings. You should train yourself to see living beings as streams. Living being is the name of a continuous stream, and all phenomena as objects of perception are only signs. Therefore, there is no real change of birth into death, or death into birth, and no person who realizes nirvāṇa.

This is also about removing the notion of self. We talk about salvation, emancipation, and liberation in terms of a self. Then we ask: who is caught in the course of transmigration? Who is going through birth and death? If you speak of reincarnation, transmigration, or rebirth, you may suppose that there must be a self that goes through birth and death. But the fact is that not only are birth and death constructions, we also do not need an agent, a subject, or a self in order for that process to continue. There is the possibility of getting out of birth and death. There is the possibility of being liberated. There is the possibility of touching nirvāṇa. But there is no self that enters nirvāṇa. So birth and death are possible without a self, and nirvāṇa is also possible without a self.

That is why removing the notion of self is so crucial. When you are caught in the notion of self, you are caught at the same time in the notion of non-self, because the self must be the opposite of what is not self. When you single something out and call it self, the rest becomes the opposite of self. So you are still caught in the double grasping of self and non-self. The first notion we have to remove is the notion of self, and all Buddhist teaching is centered on removing this notion.

◆ ◆ ◆

That does not mean that life is not there, or that the streams of living are not there. The second notion we have to remove is the notion of man. We had some discussion with Professor Tung the other day on man and non-man elements. He told us that only human beings can produce that kind of thinking, that kind of mind. But we tried to remind him that man is made only of non-man elements. Man has human ancestors, but man also has animal ancestors, vegetable ancestors, and mineral ancestors. And that is not something belonging only to the past. We continue to be animals, vegetables, and minerals at this very moment. Every time you produce a thought, even the best of thoughts, all these elements come together to help produce that thought.

So there should be no discrimination between man and non-man elements, because man is made only of non-man elements. In that way, we are free. The practice of deep ecology would not be possible without that insight of interbeing between man and non-man elements. In order to protect man, in order to help man survive, we have to do everything possible to protect non-man elements—namely animals, plants, and minerals.

The third notion we have to remove is the notion of living beings. Here, "living being" means those who are not Buddhas, not fully awakened beings. There may be a kind of complex in us: when we stand in front of the Buddha, we say that the Buddha is different from a living being. But we are taught that the Buddha is also a living being. A Buddha is made only of non-Buddha elements. Looking deeply into the person of the Buddha, we see afflictions and suffering that have been transformed into joy, happiness, and compassion. If we have not seen all these non-Buddha elements in him, we have not truly seen him yet.

It is like looking into a lotus flower. We have to see water and mud in the lotus. If you have not seen all these non-lotus elements in the lotus, you have not really seen the lotus. When you can see that, the obstacle between you and the Buddha is removed, and then there can be deep communication between you and the Buddha. This is the nondual insight that living beings and Buddha are not two different things.

We may have a similar complex in Christianity when we think that Jesus Christ is divine and immortal, while we are merely human and mortal. If we hold that view too rigidly, it becomes very difficult to have real communion and communication with Jesus Christ. So the insight of interbeing concerning living beings is very crucial.

◆ ◆ ◆

And the last notion we have to remove is the notion of lifespan. The other day we already considered this. Imagine a line representing time. We believe that we are born at one point, B, and we will die at another point, D. We believe that we begin to exist only at point B, and when we reach point D we stop existing. So the segment BD is called our lifespan. According to the teaching of the Buddha, that is not right view; that is not the absolute truth.

In the conventional realm of truth, you may speak of birth and death. You may speak of lifespan. But if you touch reality more deeply, you find that it is not correct to think in that way. We believe that the segment from B to D represents our existence, and the segment before B represents non-existence. From the realm of non-being, we have passed into the realm of being. There is supposedly a self beginning here and vanishing there. Before that, we say, we did not exist. That is our usual way of thinking.

But when we come to reality in itself, when we practice looking deeply, we see that everything is free from birth and death. There is no beginning. We have used the example of a cloud. A cloud has no beginning. When she manifests herself in the form of a cloud, that is only a continuation. Before that, she did not belong to the realm of non-being. She was already something else: the water in the ocean, the heat generated by the sun, and so on. So you cannot say that the cloud had not been there before she appeared as a cloud. The nature of the cloud is the nature of no birth.

And it is impossible for a cloud to die. In our usual way of thinking, to die means that from something you become nothing, from someone you become no one. But it is evident that a cloud cannot die. A cloud cannot pass from being into non-being. A cloud can only change form and transform. The cloud can become rain, or snow, or ice—but not nothing.

Looking deeply like that, we can remove the notion of birth and death. Not only is the nature of the cloud no birth and no death, but the nature of everything is like that. Our true nature is also the nature of no birth and no death. And if we experience that, we touch nirvāṇa, the ultimate.

◆ ◆ ◆

What is wonderful is that when you are able to remove the notion of birth and death, you can also remove the notion of being and non-being. That is very logical, because our definition of birth and death is based on the notions of being and non-being. To be born means to pass from the realm of non-being into the realm of being, and to die means to pass from the realm of being into the realm of non-being. So the notions of birth and death grow together with the notions of being and non-being. If we can remove both pairs of opposites, we are free.

Many questions can be resolved simply because we are able to remove the notions of birth and death. Then we no longer think that our life is only one hundred years long. To describe us as being is wrong, and to describe us as non-being is equally wrong. These are only notions that cannot be applied to ultimate reality.

And to me, today, it is wonderful that science has also discovered no birth and no death. According to the law of conservation of matter and energy, nothing is born and nothing dies. Everything we observe in us and around us can be described as matter and energy. Our mind is also energy, a very powerful kind of energy. Matter and mind are all forms of energy.

According to the teaching of the Buddha, there is no birth and no death, and that has been confirmed by scientists in their own way. Nothing is born; nothing can die. Suppose you perform a chemical reaction. You prepare a few substances and put them together. During the reaction, you may see the disappearance of some substance, and you may say that it is no longer there. But looking deeply, scientists will say that the total mass of all these elements before and after the reaction remains the same. Nothing is added; nothing is lost. Exactly as in the Heart Sutra: there is no increasing, there is no decreasing; nothing is lost.

So the first law of thermodynamics tells us that you cannot create energy; you can only transfer or transform it. You cannot create matter either, because the nature of matter-energy is the nature of no birth and no death. So one place where Buddhism and science can begin to meet is this insight of no birth and no death, because there is a real agreement here between the two traditions.

◆ ◆ ◆

If scientists agree that birth and death are only notions that cannot be applied to reality, then they may stop looking for an absolute beginning of the cosmos. It is a waste of time. If they stand on the firm ground of no birth and no death, they will also have to accept that being and non-being are just another pair of notions. Once you accept the absence of birth and death, you also discover that the notions of being and non-being cannot be applied to reality.

Philosophers have long asked: Why is there something rather than nothing? How can something arise out of nothing? Scientists ask the same question in another form: Where did all this come from? What is the origin of the cosmos? Is there a creator, or can the cosmos generate itself? Stephen Hawking, in The Grand Design, suggested that because there is a law like gravity, the universe can create itself out of nothing. But that is still caught in the notions of being and non-being, of birth and death.

The Buddha describes right view in terms of freedom from the notions of being and non-being. Let us begin with a sutra, the Discourse on the Middle Way. One time, when the Lord was staying in the guest house in the forest of Nālaka, the Venerable Kaccāyana came to visit him and asked, "The Tathāgata has spoken often of right view. How would the Tathāgata describe right view?"

We know that right view is one of the eight elements of the Noble Eightfold Path. And the Buddha told the venerable monk, "Venerable monk, people in the world tend to believe in one of two views: the view of being or the view of non-being. That is because they are bound to wrong perceptions. It is wrong perception that gives rise to the concepts of being and non-being.

"Kaccāyana, most people are bound to internal formations, discrimination, preference, grasping, and attachment. Those who are not bound to these internal knots of grasping and attachment no longer imagine or cling to the idea of a self. They understand, for example, that suffering comes into being when conditions are favorable, and that it fades away when conditions are no longer favorable. They no longer have any doubt. Their understanding has not come to them through others; it is their own insight. This insight is called right view."

We need time to explain this paragraph, but what we need to remember now is that right thinking should reflect that kind of insight. Right action also should reflect it. So when we ask whether the world out there is or is not, we have to remember that otherwise we may lose a great deal of time—perhaps our whole life—arguing about it. To be and not to be are two notions, and the notions of being and non-being cannot be ascribed to reality. Reality is free from both.

◆ ◆ ◆

There is a scholar who spent many years writing a book on nirvāṇa, focusing on whether nirvāṇa exists or does not exist. But nirvāṇa is ultimate reality, and to say that nirvāṇa exists is wrong. To say that nirvāṇa does not exist is equally wrong. Nirvāṇa transcends both notions of being and non-being. And not only nirvāṇa—a cloud does also, a flame does also.

So in Buddhism we prefer the word manifestation. The other day we already spoke about a flame. Before a flame manifests, we need certain conditions in order to recognize it. And when the flame manifests, we say it is there, that it exists, that it belongs to the realm of being. But before it manifests, do we really believe that the flame belongs to the realm of non-being? Is it possible for something from the realm of non-being to come into the realm of being? The fact is that before the flame manifests, we should already be free from the notions of being and non-being. To say that in this very moment the flame is not there is wrong, and to say that the flame is there is equally wrong. Looking deeply, we see that when conditions come together sufficiently, the flame can manifest. It does not come from the realm of non-being. Manifestation is different from birth.

You are a manifestation. You are not a creation. You have not come from nothing. Nothing can come from nothing, and nothing can become nothing. So the notions of being and non-being should be removed in order for us to see deeply into the true nature of reality.

Before the manifestation of the flame, we should not describe the flame in terms of being or non-being. And even after the manifestation of the flame, when we have a perception of the flame, we should not qualify it as being either. Of course, it is also free from the notion of non-being.

So this is very important. Even one line of such a sutra can already be very helpful: right understanding, right view, is free from the notions of being and non-being. In the realm of conventional truth, we speak of birth and death, being there and not being there, and we are used to it. That is why it is difficult for us to remove these notions and get in touch with the ultimate. But the ultimate is not something far away. The ultimate is right there, visible, if you have that kind of wisdom—the wisdom of non-discrimination, free from all these views.
